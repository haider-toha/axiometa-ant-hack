# Research: DeafBlind population, cost, and transport access

Compiled 2026-07-19. Every figure below carries a source URL and a confidence rating.

## Confidence ratings

- **HIGH** — official government, NHS, WHO, or a charity's own published research report
- **MEDIUM** — reputable charity webpage without a linked study, or quality journalism citing a named source
- **LOW** — anecdotal, blog, or undated

**How to read this document.** The confidence rating describes *source quality only*. It does **not** mean a figure is safe to put on a slide. A figure can be HIGH confidence and still be dangerous, because it describes a **different population** than deafblind people. Those live in "USABLE WITH CAVEAT" regardless of how good the source is. Read the caveat column before writing a line of script.

---

## THE TWO FINDINGS THAT SHAPE THE WHOLE DECK

**1. There is no published UK statistic about deafblind people and buses. None.** Searched RNIB (UK and Wales), Guide Dogs, Sense, Deafblind UK, RNID, Transport for All, NCAT, DfT and ONS. RNIB's 2025 bus report contains **zero** mentions of deafblindness or dual sensory loss (verified by full-text search of the PDF). Guide Dogs' bus report contains **zero** (same check). Transport for All: zero. NCAT: zero. DfT's National Travel Survey treats vision and hearing as **separate** categories with no combined breakout.

There is also no published UK employment rate for deafblind people, and no published UK figure for what proportion of deafblind people can travel independently.

The only genuinely deafblind-specific UK number that exists is the **population estimate**. Everything else stark in this document describes a broader population. **Say this on stage rather than borrowing a blindness figure — the gap is itself a legitimate pitch point.**

**2. The bus argument does not need a population statistic.** It stands on primary government sources alone, which is far safer. See "THE CORE ARGUMENT" below.

- **There is no separate London vs rest-of-England deadline.** Phasing is by **vehicle age only**, uniform across Great Britain. London appears in the instrument only in definitions cross-referencing the Greater London Authority Act 1999.

### ⚠️ Do NOT use these Guide Dogs figures for the "which bus has arrived" problem

**65%, 70% and 25% all describe ALIGHTING — passengers already on the bus who missed their stop.** They do not evidence the boarding problem:
- "a shocking two thirds (65%) of blind and partially sighted bus passengers have missed their stop at least once in the last six months"
- "70% told us they have at some stage missed their stop because a bus driver forgot to tell them when they had reached their destination"

Using these to argue "blind people can't tell which bus has arrived" is a **conflation of two different problems** and is the single most likely way this deck gets caught.

### Wales-only figures — commonly misattributed

**The widely-quoted "51% had difficulty finding the correct bus stop" is RNIB Cymru, Wales only — NOT the UK report.** The UK report's equivalents are different (18% / 80%). Attributing it to the UK report would be wrong.

Wales figures, verified verbatim on RNIB-owned pages but the underlying report PDF returns HTTP 403 so **sample size and fieldwork dates are unconfirmed — Confidence MEDIUM**:

- "More than half (51 per cent) of respondents said that they had difficulty finding the correct bus stop when making familiar bus journeys. This rose to 61 per cent when making unfamiliar journeys."
- "80 per cent say that audio announcements are 'sometimes, rarely, or never' present."

https://www.rnib.org.uk/news/bus-services-are-failing-their-blind-and-partially-sighted-passengers-in-wales/

TfL's Live Bus Arrivals API documentation states: **"SLAs or guaranteed support are not offered with this API."** The only accuracy-adjacent claim is unquantified marketing ("an accurate and complete information service for every one of London's 19,000 bus stops"). `EstimatedTime` is defined with no error bound, tolerance, or confidence interval. TfL's data licence disclaims accuracy: "The Information is licensed 'as is' and TfL excludes all representations, warranties, obligations and liabilities in relation to the Information to the maximum extent permitted by law." Safe phrasing: *TfL does not publish an accuracy figure for its bus arrival predictions, and its data licence explicitly disclaims all warranties as to accuracy.* https://content.tfl.gov.uk/tfl-live-bus-river-bus-arrivals-api-documentation.pdf and https://tfl.gov.uk/corporate/terms-and-conditions/transport-data-service

**Do not confuse this with TfL's Excess Wait Time / QSI metrics**, which measure how late buses run against schedule. That is a different quantity and is not prediction accuracy. https://tfl.gov.uk/corporate/publications-and-reports/buses-performance-data

**5. A TfL policy requiring drivers to announce route numbers to waiting blind passengers — not found.** Searched TfL accessible travel guidance, bus stop design guidance, passenger rights pages. **Do not assert a TfL obligation exists.** The closest verified evidence is DfT's non-binding "should" ("Drivers should be trained to recognise passengers who may struggle to identify a vehicle and to announce themselves to these passengers when appropriate" — and the same document defines "should" as "good practice, which we recommend", not a requirement) and the Guide Dogs 35% figure.

**6. "27% of registered blind and partially sighted people are in employment."** Surfaced in search snippets attributed to RNIB. **Could not be verified in any RNIB document actually fetched.** The RNIB "Enabler" summary contains no "27%" string; the RNIB 2019 employment DOCX URL returns 404. **[UNVERIFIED — do not use.]** Use the verified 42% instead. The two describe different populations — *registered* blind/partially sighted vs LFS *self-reported difficulty seeing* — which is exactly the kind of slip that gets a deck taken apart.

**7. A global headcount of deafblind people ("160 million" etc.).** WFDB publishes **percentages only**. The "around 160 million" figure appears on secondary blogs and is simply 2% of world population multiplied out by someone else. **[UNVERIFIED — do not use.]** Say "between 0.2% and 2% of the world's population", which is WFDB's own framing.

**8. WHO has no deafblindness figure.** WHO publishes vision loss and hearing loss estimates **separately**. This is itself usable: WFDB 2018 states "persons with deafblindness are not recognised as a distinct disability group. This has contributed to a persistent statistical invisibility… with only seven references to persons with deafblindness in the 2011 landmark World Report on Disability." PLOS 2023: "The global prevalence of DSI has not been fully examined."

**9. "Nearly 1 in 5 deafblind people receive no social care at all."** Search snippet attributed to Campaign to End Loneliness. Primary source not located. **[UNVERIFIED — do not use.]**

**10. Guide Dogs' "over three quarters would take the bus more frequently."** Appears on the Guide Dogs blog with no linked study and **conflicts with the primary Destination Unknown figure of 68%**. Use 68% with the primary citation; discard the "three quarters" claim.

**11. "Trapped at home."** This phrase is an Association of Optometrists headline, not an RNIB statistic. Do not quote it as one.

**12. NRCPD does not publish interpreter charge-out rates.** Worth recording as a correction. NRCPD's "fees" page lists **registration fees paid by practitioners** — RSLI £234, Trainee £142, Registered Interpreter for Deafblind People £177, Registered Lipspeaker £177. These are **not** service rates. ASLI publishes no recommended fees either. **There is no statutory or regulator-set BSL interpreter rate in the UK.** NUBSLI's union guidance is the nearest benchmark and is explicitly guidance: "The published figures are guidance, each professional decides their own fees." https://www.nrcpd.org.uk/fees

**13. NUBSLI hour definitions.** NUBSLI does not define "full day", "half day" or "short duration" in hours. **Do not state an hourly equivalent — you would be inventing the divisor.**

## THE CORE ARGUMENT (all primary government sources)

This is the sequence to lead with. It needs no population statistic and no lived-experience characterisation:

1. **The law that mandates bus announcements applies to passengers "on which they are travelling."** (SI 2023/715, reg 7) It is an **on-board** duty.
2. **DfT's own guidance concedes the problem in writing:** "Visually impaired passengers may find it difficult to identify the service they wish to join as they may not be able to read the service blinds – route number and end destination," and this "is amplified at bus and coach stops served by multiple routes where services may 'stack up'."
3. **DfT's remedy for that — external speakers — is filed under "further steps you can choose to take."** It is voluntary.
4. **The mandatory on-board duty is not fully phased in until 1 October 2026**, and "partially compliant" vehicles have until **1 October 2031**. As of 2021–22 only **46%** of English buses had the equipment at all — **25% outside London**.
5. **The human workaround fails:** only **35%** of blind and partially sighted passengers said drivers always tell them the bus number *even when asked* (Guide Dogs, 2014, n=818) — down from 55% the year before.

**One-line version:** *The law requires a bus to announce its route number when the doors open — but only to the people already inside it. Announcing it to the person standing at the stop is optional.*

## NOTES FOR THE SCRIPT WRITER

### Phrasings that would be wrong

- ❌ **"82% of deafblind people are unemployed."** Two errors: wrong population ("complex disabilities" = any two of sight loss, hearing loss, autism, learning disability), and "unemployed" there means *not in employment*. The tell is that the same table counts 23% of non-disabled people as "unemployed".
- ❌ **"53% of deafblind people can't leave the house."** It is an **odds ratio** (OR = 0.47) from a **US** study of people **aged 65+**.
- ❌ **"65% of blind people have missed their bus because they couldn't tell which one it was."** That 65% is about **missing your stop while already on the bus**. Different problem. This is the most likely conflation to get caught.
- ❌ **"51% of blind people can't find the right bus stop."** That is **Wales only** (RNIB Cymru). The UK report's numbers are different.
- ❌ **"There are 160 million deafblind people worldwide."** Nobody authoritative publishes that.
- ❌ **"Buses have to announce the route number so blind people know which bus has arrived."** They must announce it **to people already on board**, at the moment the doors open.
- ❌ **"London buses have no external announcements."** No source found either way. Do not claim it.
- ❌ **"A guide dog costs £55,000."** Stale, and less than the £77,000 training cost alone. Use £102,000.
- ❌ **"It costs £25,000 a year to support a guide dog."** The £25,000 covers the **entire working life** of the partnership. Guide Dogs publishes no annual figure.

### Phrasings that are safe

- ✅ "Sense estimates over 450,000 people in the UK are deafblind, rising to over 610,000 by 2035."
- ✅ "The UK government's own definition of deafblindness names three difficulties: communication, access to information, and **mobility**." Strong opener — the official definition already contains the problem the device addresses.
- ✅ "The law requires the route number to be announced to passengers travelling on the service. Announcing it to someone waiting at the stop is optional."
- ✅ "In 2014 Guide Dogs found only 35% of drivers always told a blind passenger the bus number when asked."
- ✅ "RNIB's 2025 report says many blind and partially sighted people find it difficult to determine if the arriving bus is the one they need." (Qualitative — do not attach a percentage.)

### Arithmetic warning on the guide dog comparison

3,197 guide dog partnerships vs 450,000 deafblind people is a tempting slide and **not a like-for-like comparison** — guide dogs serve blind and partially sighted people generally, and most guide dog owners are not deafblind. The honest version is a **cost** comparison: one guide dog partnership costs £102,000 over its life. Do not imply 3,197 is the supply of guide dogs *for deafblind people*.

### Use the right slice of the population

Only **125,452** of the 451,211 are aged 20–69; **302,380** are 70+. If the pitch describes a working-age commuter, cite 125,452. If it describes an older person travelling to the shops, 450,000 is fine and the age skew is *supporting* evidence.

### The 450,000 figure is a model, not a count — always say "estimated"

Sense's own method note: "These data are based on research commissioned by Sense and delivered by the Operational Research Society in 2017. This research used prevalence estimates from previous research completed by the Centre for Disability Research in 2010 and population estimates from the ONS."

Chain: **2010 prevalence work → modelled forward 2017 → applied to 2022 ONS population.** Best figure available, and every UK deafblind charity uses it — Deafblind UK's *Annual Report 2024–2025* repeats "Over 450,000 people are affected by deafblindness in the UK" — but that is the **same underlying estimate**, not independent corroboration. If a judge asks where it comes from: Sense / Operational Research Society 2017, on Centre for Disability Research 2010 prevalence.

### An audibility fix does not help a DeafBlind user — make this explicit

RNIB's finding that 55% say announcements "are not always loud or clear enough to be useful" is about **volume**. Every remedy in the DfT guidance is audio-plus-visual. For someone with both sight and hearing loss, **louder and brighter both fail**. That is the gap a tactile channel fills, and it is worth saying plainly, because a judge may otherwise ask "won't the 2026 regulations solve this?" The answer is no — the regulations mandate exactly the two channels a DeafBlind person cannot use.

### London specifics, if the demo names a London route

DfT: "For over 15 years, most bus services in London have incorporated audible and visible information on the route and upcoming stops." TfL's iBus announces route and destination on boarding (e.g. "177, to, Peckham"), and TfL's press release quotes Guide Dogs London: "The **on-board** audio information means that a greater number of blind people are able to travel independently and orientate themselves throughout their journey" (30 April 2019). https://tfl.gov.uk/info-for/media/press-releases/2019/april/ten-years-of-tfl-s-ibus-has-given-greater-independence-to-customers-with-hearing-or-visual-impairments

**Careful:** London is the *best-served* case in the country. Do not pick a London example to argue that provision is absent — argue instead that even where provision is best, it is on-board and audio-visual, so it still does not reach a DeafBlind person standing at the stop.

### Deafblindness is statistically invisible — and that is itself a slide

If a judge asks why the numbers are thin, the honest answer is also the argument. WFDB 2018: "persons with deafblindness are not recognised as a distinct disability group. This has contributed to a persistent statistical invisibility… with only seven references to persons with deafblindness in the 2011 landmark World Report on Disability." PLOS 2023: "The global prevalence of DSI has not been fully examined." DfT's National Travel Survey has no dual-sensory category. RNIB's 1,197-person bus survey has no deafblind breakout. Guide Dogs' 2,009-person survey has none. Transport for All's 521 have none. NCAT's 1,195 have none.

The absence of data is not a weakness in the research. It is the finding.
